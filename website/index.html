The Problem
LiDAR is either accurate or affordable. Never both.
Depth perception is fundamental to any robot navigating physical space. Industrial LiDAR delivers dense, reliable depth — at $8,000–$75,000 per unit.[1]

Apple disrupted the cost curve. The iPhone 15 Pro ships a solid-state LiDAR scanner at roughly $3–$25 in component cost.[2] But the output is sparse, only 192×256 pixels — far too low-resolution for reliable scene understanding.[3]

SPECTRA closes this gap. We take the sparse, low-resolution depth from a cheap consumer LiDAR and produce a dense, full-resolution metric depth map — bringing sub-$30 hardware to tasks that previously required a $10,000 rig. The key insight: these small LiDAR modules do not need to be embedded in iPhones — any $10 solid-state LiDAR paired with a camera works with our model.
Industrial LiDAR (Velodyne, Ouster)
$10,000+

The Gap SPECTRA Closes
Sparse depth → unusable
for robotics without SPECTRA
Raw consumer LiDAR misses thin objects, bleeds across edges, and lacks the spatial resolution for path planning, grasping, or scene understanding. [3]
Our Solution
Dense indoor depth from sparse consumer LiDAR.
SPECTRANet is a lightweight neural network trained on ARKitScenes with Faro laser scanner ground truth. [4] It fuses sparse LiDAR depth with RGB to produce dense, edge-aligned, metric depth maps.

Scoped to the 0.5–10m indoor range — where home robotics operates. The full model runs on-device via CoreML at ~2MB, with zero cloud latency. The same approach applies to any cheap RGB+LiDAR pair, not just iPhones.

Home Robotics Deep Dive
Raw consumer LiDAR vs SPECTRA — same scene, same robot
The Roomba relies on 2D infrared and bump sensors — no 3D depth. [5] A $10 solid-state LiDAR module gives it depth, but only if that depth is dense enough to see a sock, a chair leg, or a pet. [3]
Raw LiDAR — 192×256 sparse · 10 scan rays
Sparse rays miss thin obstacles (chair legs, toys, wires). Robot collides with objects that simply fall between scan lines — shown by collision indicator.
SPECTRA dense — 768×1024 · 60 rays equivalent
Dense depth map detects every obstacle. The robot plans a clean path around all objects including ones the raw LiDAR would never detect.
